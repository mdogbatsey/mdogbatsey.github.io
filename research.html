Research Overview

My research lies at the intersection of Scientific Computing, Mathematical Modeling, and Mathematical Biology.

Focus Areas

My work involves developing and implementing advanced numerical methods, such as finite difference schemes, Matched Interface and Boundary (MIB) methods, and Finite Element Methods (FEM). I apply these methods to solve multidimensional partial differential equations (PDEs) on high-performance computing (HPC) platforms for real-world applications in drug design and protein engineering. I'm also interested in social dynamics modeling, using mathematics to study complex systems like crime and gangs.

  • Mathematical Biology: Implicit solvation models, biomolecular solvation analysis, ensemble averaged electrostatics, mathematical methods for molecular biology, and PDE modeling of molecular surfaces.
  • Scientific Computing: Electrostatic analysis, material interface problems, and biomolecular simulations.
  • Numerical Methods for PDEs: Matched Interface and Boundary (MIB) method and Alternating Direction Implicit (ADI) methods.
  • Mathematical Modeling: Social dynamics modeling (e.g., Gang dynamics).

Current Projects

Biomolecular Solvation

I am developing a Variational Implicit Solvation Model that incorporates:

  • Size-Modified Poisson-Boltzmann (SMPB) theory.
  • Diffuse Interface approaches.
  • Ensemble averaged electrostatics for accurate free energy calculations.

High-Order Numerical Schemes

Developing robust solvers for interface problems and stiff systems:

  • 3D p-Laplace Equations: Finite difference methods for solving nonlinear PDEs in three dimensions.
  • ADI Methods: Efficient time-stepping for parabolic equations.
  • MIB Method: Handling complex geometry and material interfaces in solvation models.

Social Dynamics Modeling

Beyond physics-based modeling, I apply ODE systems to understand social phenomena. My recent work assesses the impact of intervention programs on gang dynamics using mathematical modeling approaches.

Selected Visualizations

Electrostatic Analysis: Electrostatic surface potential of the protein (PDB ID: 451c) mapped onto surface generated after solving the p-Laplace equation (with p = 1.2). Visualization was done in VMD.
Numerical Scheme Efficiency: Comparison of three schemes (ADI, Crank-Nicolson, and Picard's Iteration) for solving the p-Laplace equation across 23 proteins. The results demonstrate that the ADI method is computationally the most efficient.
Social Dynamics: A model showing how crime and criminal activities spread in a community.
